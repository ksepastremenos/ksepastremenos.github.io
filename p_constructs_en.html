▸ Constructions, Mechanisms & Experiments

This is a personal collection of constructions, installations, and standalone projects. There isn’t a thematic connection between them; some were created as part of assignments during my master’s studies, others for exhibitions, some for purely practical purposes, and others simply in order to experiment with new technologies.

The "Automatic Television" is a handcrafted toy made from MDF, polypropylene pipes, a bicycle tube, cardboard, magnets, bearings, metal hardware, LEGO, and microelectronics. Inspired by the archetype of the handmade cardboard TV using kitchen rolls, and the wooden TVs of the 1970s, it tells a story about the relationship between the painter, artificial intelligence, and the commodification of art.

The "MIDI Furniture" is also based on the aesthetic of retro wooden electronic devices. It's a MIDI controller built with an Arduino that sends data via a DIN cable. In the demo video, it communicates with a sound card and a script written in SuperCollider.

This piece also inspired the creation of small modular boxes that can hold up to four potentiometers or buttons. These were later used in an open workshop on robotics and the MIDI protocol.

Additionally, it inspired the creation of a relay system was to allow remote switching of audio inputs on a sound card.

"Chaos Behind Your Back" is a kinetic sculptuire made primarily from recycled materials: wood from old furniture, springs from a used mattress, a bicycle wheel, and a broken electric coffee stirrer. It comments on the normalization of the lecture-as-monologue in the Greek educational system.

"The City is Magic" is a stop-motion animation, with each frame physically placed in public spaces around Thessaloniki. The frames were created in Blender and printed on corrugated cardboard, before being printed onto public spaces around the city.

The bed for the Hyundai Matrix (2004) is a custom-built structure made from plywood, beams, and detachable metal legs, turning the car into a camping vehicle. Photogrammetry was used to accurately calculate and optimize the available space.
